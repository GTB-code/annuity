The PILL framework

Four guarantees. In writing.

Most retirement plans run on maybe — back-tested averages, simulated futures, hopeful assumptions. An annuity runs on contract. Here's what's actually promised, in the four ways it shows up in your life.

PILL
P

Principal protection.

The dollars you put in cannot decrease due to market losses. Not in the worst quarter. Not in the worst decade. Your contract value is set when you sign — and the issuer is legally on the hook for it.

Contractual clause
"Account value will not decrease due to negative index performance."
I

Income for life.

A specific dollar amount, deposited every month, for as long as you're alive. Not "expected." Not "projected." Promised. If you live to 105, the check still arrives on the first.

Contractual clause
"Lifetime income payments will continue for the life of the annuitant."
L

Legacy.

What's left when you're gone goes to the people you choose, not back to the insurance company. Named beneficiaries. Direct payment. No probate, no waiting, no clawback.

Contractual clause
"Death benefit paid directly to designated beneficiaries."
L

Long-term care.

If you need home care, assisted living, or nursing care, your monthly payment doubles — typically for up to five years. Built into the contract. No separate LTC policy. No medical underwriting at claim time.

Contractual clause
"Income payments increase 2× upon qualifying care event."

This isn't a forecast. It's a contract — backed by an insurance company's reserves and your state's guaranty association.

No Monte Carlo. No "84% chance." Just four guarantees, in writing, signed.

M

Margaret, 65. Just retired from a 33-year teaching career.

$680,000 in retirement accounts. Pension covers $2,100/mo. Social Security at 67 covers $2,400/mo. Wants to spend $5,500/mo without checking the markets.

The fear

Margaret retired in May 2008. The friend who retired with her watched her portfolio drop 40% over the next ten months, then spent the first decade of her retirement working part-time at the library to make up the gap. Margaret doesn't want a Monte Carlo simulation telling her there's an 84% chance she'll be okay. She wants to know what will hit her account on the first of each month.

The strategy

Move $200,000 into a single-premium immediate annuity (SPIA), life-only with cash refund — so if she passes early, beneficiaries get the unpaid balance. The remaining $480K stays invested for growth, legacy, and discretionary spending. The annuity covers the $1,000/mo gap between guaranteed income and her spending floor.

D

David, 58. Just got the early-retirement package.

$1.2M in 401(k). Severance covers 12 months. Doesn't plan to work again. Healthy, marathon runner, dad lived to 94. Doesn't want to start drawing down for 7 years.

The fear

David is "rich on paper, poor in income." His 401(k) is huge but every dollar is exposed. He's 27 years from his life expectancy and possibly 36 years from when he'll actually die. Sequence-of-returns risk in his early sixties could blow up the whole plan. He doesn't need income today. He needs to know income will be there at 65 — locked in at today's rates, before they change.

The strategy

Buy a Deferred Income Annuity (DIA) today with $250K, with payments starting at 65. The insurer holds the money for 7 years, invests it, and bears all the risk. David knows — to the penny — what hits his account starting his 65th birthday. The remaining $950K stays invested for growth and pre-65 spending.

L+R

Linda & Robert, both 67. One year into retirement.

$890K combined IRAs. Two SS checks total $4,800/mo. No pension. Kids are grown. Want $7,500/mo to live comfortably + cover Linda's mom's potential care needs.

The fear

Linda watched her mother's savings get drained by three years of memory care at $9,200/mo. $331K, gone. She doesn't want her kids to face that decision about her, and she doesn't want to gamble on whether the market is up or down on the day a long-term care bill arrives. She also wants Robert taken care of if she goes first, and vice versa. They want one contract that solves both.

The strategy

Convert $400,000 into a joint-life SPIA with a long-term care doubler rider. Income continues for as long as either spouse is alive. If either qualifies for LTC under contract triggers (2 of 6 ADLs or cognitive impairment), monthly payments double for up to 5 years. The remaining $490K stays invested.
